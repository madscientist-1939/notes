ADDITION AND SUBTRACTION OF SMALL NUMBERS
==========================================

-> Math began very simply as part of our language with addition and subtraction.

-> Arithmetics: The first kind of math developed by the human race. It deals with 
counting numbers and methods to manipulate them. This type of math came from 
questions like 'How much food we need to survive this winter?', 'How many people are in the tribe?' and so on. This type of math came from practical applications.

-- Addition

-> Addition: It represents the combination of two or more numbers into one number,
called a 'sum'. For example, having two apples and adding another two apples, we then
combine all of them into four apples:
    2 + 2 = 4
    These symbols represent this process: adding two apples to two apples will give
    you four apples in total(sum).

-> The symbols that we saw right above are combines into one mathematical object,
called 'equation'. An equation is an affirmation of equality: We're affirming that
the part on the left(2 + 2) is equal to the part on the right(4). It is important to 
know that an equation MUST HAVE an equal sign(=). Without it, this object cannot be
considered an equation. For example, using only the symbols 2 + 2 does not define 
an equation, only a mathematical expression.

-> 2 + 2 = 4 reads as 'two plus two equals four', or 'two and two is four'. This is 
just a symbolic way of representing the idea of adding two object together and 
obtaining four objects. It is a much more concise way of writing this idea.

-- Subtraction

-> Subtraction: Opposite of addition. It doesn't find the sum of two or more numbers,
it finds their difference. For example: You have five apples and you eat one of them.
How many do you have left? This equation can be represented as:
    5 - 1 = 4
    Which essentially means that 'five less one is four'. Since we're talking
    about differences, the number four represents the difference between the 
    numbers five and one.

-- Relevant Properties

1. Commutation
    Addition have one property called 'commutative property'. This property tells
    us that the order of numbers on the left part of the equation does not matter:
    the left part will still be the same. For example, 3 + 4 = 7 is the same as 
    writing 4 + 3 = 7. Notice how both equations equal to 7.

    Subtraction on the other hand does not have this property. We can easily see
    this by trying: 4 - 3 = 1 and 3 - 4 = -1, i.e. the order of the left part of
    the equation does matter. 

2. Association
    Addition has another property called 'associative property'. This property 
    tell us that in a mathematical expression with addition, it does not matter
    which collection of numbers we decide to add first, the result will still 
    be the same. For example: 2 + 3 + 5 will equal 10 if we add 2 + 3 and then
    + 5, or if we add 3 + 5 and then 2.

    Subtraction also do not have this property.
